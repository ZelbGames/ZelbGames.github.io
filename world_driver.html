World Driver

Abstract

World Driver is a technical demo written in C++ and Gdscript using the Godot engine.

The game produces a 1:1 scale representation of earthâ€™s roads (accurate to 1cm), places the player behind the wheel of a car and sets them up with a timing system. This offers them the freedom to create their own challenges and objectives in a safe and open sandbox environment.

The initial motivation behind the game was to facilitate friendly competition between my friends and I based around how long it took for each of us to complete common routes.

I created a game that applied real world (Geo-Json) data, sourced from the OpenStreetMap (OSM) Database, to a bespoke 3D virtual environment. As a player I can place myself on any road on earth and potentially drive to any other connecting road. I have access to a suite of timing tools to record times taken to travel between points. The project proved successful in that my friends and I were able to enjoy comparing times and taking part in light competition.

Structure

The game accesses live mapping data from OSM servers, from which geometric data for each road is stored locally in a dictionary as an array of coordinates. A C++ DLL manages the sourcing of this data, translating the latitudinal/ longitudinal coordinates into a cartesian coordinate system based on the web sMercator projection and ultimately scaling each relative to the origin. In the Godot engine the road data for a 10 square kilometres area around the player is requested through the DLL and plotted as Godot 3D paths. A second DLL calculates control points for each path, creating individual quadratic Bezier curves to represent the roads. When the player travels too far from the origin (1.25km) the origin coordinates shift, effectively splitting the world into a set of 2.5 square kilometres chunks. The player explores the environment through a bespoke car controller with time keeping capabilities.
